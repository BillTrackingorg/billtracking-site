Why a feed, in a course

The textbook explains the process. The feed shows it running.

Every diagram of "how a bill becomes a law" is static. The feeds are that same diagram executing in real time: cloture motions failing, trilogue mandates landing, vetoes surviving or falling — during your semester, on bills your students have opinions about. This week's posts are next week's discussion material, and every post cites the official record, so it can be assigned, quoted and checked.

The record was never the problem

Congress.gov and the EU's registers have been free and public for decades — and students rarely open them unassigned. The gap was never access; it's salience. A database is where information waits; a feed is where information arrives. Students can find the legislative record, but often prefer the record to find them instead. Readership isn't always decided by where information resides — it's decided by how information flows, and feeds are how information flows today. The official record belongs in the flow, not only in the archive.

Getting started — three steps, no forms

  1. Follow the feed that fits your course — or both: @USBillTracker · @EUBillTracker.
  2. Put it in front of students — link a feed from the syllabus or LMS, or drop a ready-made follow slide into your week-one deck (all copy-paste material is in the classroom resources below).
  3. Optional: run the verification exercise — a ready one-page assignment where students audit the feed against the official record.

What we're asking: nothing beyond a follow, and only if it fits your course. There's no mailing list, no partnership program, no upsell. The project grows when educators who find it useful mention it to students and colleagues — that's the entire model.

A ready-made assignment

The verification exercise

The feeds are built to be checked — which makes them a genuine methods exercise, not busywork:

  1. Pick a post. Each student takes one update from either feed — a committee report, a cloture vote, a trilogue deal.
  2. Pull the record. Find the underlying entry on Congress.gov or in the OEIL procedure file the post cites.
  3. Audit every claim. Dates, vote counts, stage labels, "what's next" — does the post say exactly what the record supports, no more?
  4. Report what you find. Discrepancies go to accuracy@billtracking.org. Confirmed reports are fixed visibly and listed in the public corrections log — with the student credited by name, if they opt in.

A credited correction is a linkable artifact. A student who catches a real error in a public record gets a permanent, citable entry in the corrections log — something concrete for a CV or graduate application. We'd genuinely rather be corrected by your students than stay quietly wrong. Group audits are welcome too — findings can be credited to a course as a whole.
