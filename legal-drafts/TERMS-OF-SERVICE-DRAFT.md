## 2. What the Service is

BillTracking publishes the **official legislative record** of the United States Congress
and of the European Union institutions:

- Two accounts on X post updates as bills move through the legislative process.
- The mobile app shows those same updates, plus each bill's recorded history.
- Readers may optionally record a vote — an expression of support or opposition — on a
  bill. Vote results are shown in aggregate.

Source material is drawn from official public sources, including Congress.gov and the EU's
OEIL, Cellar and European Parliament open-data services.

The Service is independent and non-commercial. It has no sponsors, no advertising, no paid
placements and no subscriptions. Its only revenue is X creator monetization.

## 6. Reader voting — acceptable use

Voting is optional. You can read everything on the Service without ever voting.

**How votes work.** A vote is submitted **anonymously**. It carries no account, no user
identifier and no device identifier, because the Service has none. A coarse geographic value
(country, and US state for US readers) is attached so that results can be shown by place.

**The consequences of that design, which you should understand before voting:**

- **A vote cannot be changed or withdrawn once submitted.** There is nothing tying the vote
  to you, so there is no way for us to find your vote in order to edit or delete it. This is
  a deliberate privacy choice, not a limitation we have failed to fix.
- **There is no prevention of repeat voting.** For the same reason, we cannot tell whether
  two votes came from the same person. Results are therefore an indication of reader
  sentiment among people using this app — **they are not a survey, not a poll, and not a
  statistically valid measurement of opinion.** Do not cite them as one.
- **Small groups are hidden.** Where too few people have voted on a bill in a given place,
  the breakdown is withheld and only a total is shown, so that a handful of readers' views
  cannot be exposed.

**Acceptable use.** When using the Service you agree not to:

- submit votes automatically, in bulk, or through any script, bot or tool, or otherwise
  attempt to distort vote results;
- attempt to identify, single out or re-identify any individual reader from published
  aggregate results, or to combine published results with other data for that purpose;
- attempt to access, probe, interfere with or disrupt the Service, its infrastructure or its
  data sources, including by scraping at a rate that burdens them;
- copy or redistribute the Service's presentation, layout, written summaries or design in a
  way that misrepresents them as an official government product, or as your own;
- present the Service, or content taken from it, as an official record, as endorsed by any
  institution, or as legal advice;
- use the Service in violation of any law that applies to you.

We may withhold, correct or remove vote results that we reasonably believe have been
manipulated. Because there are no accounts, we have no means of suspending an individual
user, and we do not claim to.

> ## 🔵 OPEN QUESTION — GOVERNING LAW AND JURISDICTION. **NOT DECIDED. DO NOT PUBLISH THIS

>
> This draft deliberately **does not pick** a governing law or forum, because the choice is
> genuinely contested here and picking one badly is worse than leaving it to advice.
>
> The relevant facts, which counsel needs:
>
> - The publishing company is **registered in Kenya**.
> - The owner/operator is **resident in Poland** (EU).
> - The users are **in the United States and the European Union**, and are targeted there
>   deliberately (US and EU law-school audiences, an explicit EU tab, EU institutional data
>   sources).
> - The Service is **free** and users are overwhelmingly **consumers**, not businesses.
>
> The tensions counsel must resolve:
>
> 1. **A choice-of-law clause will not fully work against EU consumers.** Under the Rome I
>    Regulation, a choice of law in a consumer contract cannot deprive the consumer of the
>    protection of the mandatory rules of their country of habitual residence, where the
>    trader directs activities to that country. This Service plainly directs activities at
>    EU Member States. So naming Kenyan law will not displace, say, Polish or Irish consumer
>    protection for those users.
>    <https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=celex%3A32008R0593>
> 2. **A forum-selection clause has the same problem.** Under the Brussels I Recast
>    Regulation, a consumer domiciled in a Member State may generally sue, and can generally
>    only be sued, in their own Member State, and jurisdiction agreements departing from
>    that are tightly restricted.
>    <https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=celex%3A32012R1215>
> 3. **US users are a separate analysis.** US courts more readily enforce choice-of-law and
>    forum clauses in online terms, but enforceability depends on how the terms are
>    presented and accepted, and some state consumer statutes resist waiver.
> 4. **Where does the operator actually want to be sued, and where could a judgment reach
>    him?** Realistically the operator's assets and residence are the practical answer, and
>    that points at Poland, not Kenya — which is an argument for not fighting a battle the
>    clause cannot win.
> 5. **Does the entity choice change?** If the publishing entity question in §1 is resolved
>    differently (e.g. a Polish or other EU entity), this section changes with it. Resolve
>    §1 first.
>
> A plausible shape, for counsel to accept or reject — **not a recommendation from this
> draft**: name one governing law for the terms generally, add an express carve-out stating
> that consumers resident in the EU keep the mandatory protections and the courts of their
> country of residence, and say nothing that purports to strip a US user of a
> non-waivable state-law right. Counsel decides.
>
> Whatever is chosen must be consistent with the privacy policy, which is governed by the
> GDPR regardless of any choice-of-law clause — GDPR territorial scope under Article 3(2)
> cannot be contracted out of.
> <https://gdpr-info.eu/art-3-gdpr/>
