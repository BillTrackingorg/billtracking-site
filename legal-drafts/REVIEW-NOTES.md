**Audiences:** Thomas Vanhoutte (operator, non-lawyer) and the lawyer reviewing the drafts.

## 1. What this is

These are AI-prepared drafts of a Privacy Policy and Terms of Use for BillTracking, written from a code audit of the app, the site and the two bots. **They are not legal advice and no part of them has been reviewed by a lawyer.** Nothing in this package should be published until counsel has reviewed it and every inline `[[PLACEHOLDER]]` and blockquote note has been resolved and removed.

## 2. ⚠️ What changed in this rewrite, and why

The earlier drafts described a product that does not exist. They were written from an unverified mental model of the system rather than from the code. This set is written from an audit with file:line evidence behind every factual claim. Three corrections matter most:

**a) Reader voting is NOT BUILT. It is user-interface only.**
There is no vote submission of any kind. Tapping a vote chip writes to in-memory state and to the local storage blob on the device. The counts shown are a hardcoded sample constant plus the reader's own tap; the geographic breakdown is generated by a seeded pseudo-random number generator. Nothing is transmitted, received, aggregated or stored server-side — there is no server. Earlier text describing votes being "submitted anonymously", "collected" or "aggregated" described a system that does not exist. All such language is now future-tense and marked as planned, or removed.

**b) The site has NO analytics. A GA4 disclosure had been included in error.**
The previous draft disclosed Google Analytics 4 on billtracking.org. That was imported from an unrelated project and was simply false. The site runs zero JavaScript, has no `<script>`, no `<iframe>`, no `<form>`, no third-party resources at all — fonts are self-hosted woff2, everything else is same-origin. There is no consent banner because nothing on the site requires consent. Publishing a GA4 disclosure would have been a false statement in the one document whose purpose is not containing one.

**c) The app makes ZERO network requests and ships no SDKs.**
No fetch, no axios, no XHR, no WebSocket anywhere in `src/`. No API base URL, no endpoint constant. Absence of Sentry, Firebase, Amplitude, Segment, Google Analytics, the Facebook SDK, expo-insights and expo-updates was verified against installed `node_modules` **and** the lockfile, not just the manifest. `expo-notifications` is not installed, so no push token can exist. The app generates and transmits no device id, install id or UUID.

**Why this matters beyond accuracy:** the audience for these documents is law professors and students. A single checkable false statement — a disclosed analytics tool that is not installed, a described vote pipeline that does not exist — costs more credibility than the entire rest of the document earns.

**One further false statement was removed:** earlier product copy asserted that "our only revenue is X creator monetization". The project earns nothing and never has. There are no ads, sponsors, in-app purchases, subscriptions or paid placements. X creator monetization requires a subscriber threshold the accounts are nowhere near (53 subscribers across both feeds) — the project is not eligible and not enrolled. Do not reintroduce that claim in any form. Equally, the drafts deliberately do **not** assert the legal conclusion "non-commercial", because that word does real work in CalOPPA, CCPA and EU trader/consumer analysis. The facts are stated; counsel classifies.

## 3. The one thing that matters most

**A reader's vote on a bill is a political opinion. Under GDPR Article 9 that is special-category data — the most heavily restricted class there is, alongside health and biometrics.**

In plain English: ordinary personal data needs a lawful basis under Article 6. Special-category data needs that *and* a separate condition under Article 9(2), and the available conditions are narrow. This is why the vote feature is the single highest-risk element of the whole product, despite looking like the most trivial.

**Why the anonymous design exists.** The intended design submits a vote with no user id, no device id and no account — the server would receive a bill reference and a position, and would have no way to link two votes to the same person. If that holds, the submitted data is not personal data at all, the Article 9 problem does not arise, and there is nothing to breach, subpoena or leak.

**What it buys.** No identifier to protect. No database of who holds which political view. No meaningful target for a data request. Nothing to hand over if compelled.

**What it costs, and these are real losses:**
- **No withdrawal or correction.** Once a vote is submitted there is no way to find it again, so a reader cannot change or delete it. There is nothing to delete *because* there is nothing linking it to them.
- **No double-vote prevention.** The same person can vote repeatedly from the same device or several devices, and the system cannot tell.
- **No vote history.** A reader cannot see what they voted on across devices.
- **Weak fraud resistance.** Any published tally is soft evidence and should be presented as reader sentiment, never as a poll or a survey.

**And none of it is built.** This is a design commitment to honour when the feature is built — not a present fact to disclose. The documents must not describe it in the present tense.

**Two consequences worth registering now:**

- Two of the items currently persisted on the device are GDPR-sensitive even though they never leave it: the vote map (political opinion, Article 9 material) and the free-text search keywords (arbitrarily revealing). Whether the operator is a "controller" for data written to a user's own device by software he authored but cannot read is a genuinely contested question — counsel question 11 below.
- **The cheapest available fix in the entire package:** do not persist the vote map at all before a backend exists. Hold it in memory only. That is roughly one line of code, and it removes the whole Article 9 and ePrivacy Article 5(3) discussion from the Privacy Policy. See decision 2.

## 4. 🔵 Code that must change before these documents can be published

Two latent defects are currently *disclosed* in the drafts. Fixing them deletes the disclosure and removes a defect from the public record.

### 4.1 Small-cell suppression leaks by differencing

**What exists.** `MIN_CELL = 10` in one UI component. Where a geographic cell has fewer than ten entries it withholds the up/down split, while **always** showing the row total.

**What is wrong — two separate defects:**
1. **Re-evaluated per view and recomputed every render.** Suppression is not decided once and applied stickily. A category hidden at nine becomes visible at ten, so the transition itself is informative.
2. **Differencing leak.** Row up-counts sum exactly to the published total. Subtract the visible splits from the published total and the hidden ones fall out arithmetically. Suppression achieves nothing against anyone who does the subtraction.

**Why it blocks a published claim.** Any sentence describing this as a safeguard that protects readers would be false. Today it protects synthetic data and exposes nobody — every number in the app is fabricated — but the algorithm as written would leak if wired to a real backend unchanged. Do not describe it as a working safeguard. If mentioned at all, describe it as a *planned* disclosure control and do not promise that it works.

**Roughly what fixing involves.** Decide suppression once, over the full result set, and apply that decision consistently across every derived view and every re-render — not independently per view. Then close the arithmetic: either suppress the row total alongside the split whenever the split is suppressed, or pool suppressed cells into a single "other" bucket so the published totals no longer reconcile to the visible rows. This is a design change to the aggregation layer, not a threshold tweak. It should be done before a real backend ships, not after.

**Documentation note:** the working description of *how* to defeat the control (the differencing mechanics, the per-render recomputation, the Cohen & Nissim citation) belongs here and in a code comment — **not** in the published Privacy Policy or Terms. Today it is harmless because the data is fake; the risk is that the paragraph survives into the version published on the day a real backend ships, at which point it is an exploitation guide with the operator's name on it. Keep one plain public sentence in each document: *small-cell suppression exists in the interface but operates on sample data and is not a working privacy safeguard; do not rely on it.*

### 4.2 The site's permalink generator strips the AI label

**What exists.** AI-generated bill summaries carry a literal `[AI-Generated]` label. In the app the label is structurally coupled to the summary — a summary cannot render without it — and both bots' composers append it.

**What is wrong.** The site's permalink generator writes the AI summary into the page `<meta name="description">` and into the Open Graph / Twitter card tags **without the label**. So Google results and every social preview show AI-written text with no marker. This is unfixed.

**Why it blocks a published claim.** It means the drafts cannot say "AI text always carries the label" — that sentence would be false. It is also the one EU AI Act Article 50 gap that is squarely within the operator's control (see 4.2a).

**Roughly what fixing involves.** Small: in the permalink generator, use the labelled string when building meta description and OG/Twitter card values, or prepend the marker at the point of serialisation. Watch the meta-description length limit — the label costs characters, so truncate after prepending, not before. Once fixed, the disclosure can be deleted from both documents.

**4.2a — a scope limit not to overstate.** Several post types are assembled purely from the official record with no model involvement and correctly carry no label. The accurate claim is **"AI-generated summaries are labelled"**, never "every post is labelled".

**4.2b — AI Act note for counsel, not for the published text.** The second subparagraph of Article 50(4) exempts AI-generated content that has undergone human review or editorial control where a natural or legal person holds editorial responsibility for its publication. The bots publish unreviewed on a cron schedule, so we do **not** rely on that exemption — which is precisely why the meta-tag gap matters rather than being a technicality. If a human review step is ever introduced, revisit.

### 4.3 Also blocking, though not code defects

- **Privacy manifest / required-reason API.** The draft asserted that the app's local storage is backed by `NSUserDefaults` on iOS, making a privacy manifest declaration necessary. **That assertion is very likely wrong** — React Native / Expo AsyncStorage on iOS is a file-based store, not `NSUserDefaults`. This is not in the audit and it drives an actual Apple filing, so a wrong answer propagates into a store submission. Verify against the installed async-storage implementation in `node_modules` before filing anything, and declare accordingly.
- **Clipboard read.** The audit establishes that the app *writes* to the clipboard on user action. It does not establish the absence of any *read*. Verify there are no `getStringAsync` / `getString` call sites in `src/` before publishing the sentence "it never reads the clipboard". iOS shows a system toast on clipboard reads, so a false negative here is visible to users. If not verified before publication, delete the sentence — the write disclosure stands on its own.
- **How legislative content gets into the app.** The drafts state it is "bundled at build time". The audit establishes zero network requests and that all *images* are bundled local assets; it says nothing about the content pipeline. Either verify the pipeline or reduce to the audited fact: *the app makes no network requests, so any content it shows is already inside the app package.*
- **Privacy policy URL and in-app link.** Both stores require the policy at a public URL *and* reachable from inside the app (App Store Connect metadata plus Apple Guideline 5.1.1; the Play Console policy link requirement). The site is not deployed, so the URL does not exist, and no in-app Privacy Policy link screen has been built. This is a build task hiding inside a placeholder — the app cannot be submitted until it is done. Also confirm the App Store Connect support URL, which neither draft mentions.
- **Access-control check (two minutes).** The drafts claim the operator is the only person with access to the mailbox and the repositories. That is an access-control fact, not a code fact, and nobody has checked it. Check the GitHub collaborator lists on both repos and Exchange mailbox delegation, then either keep the claim or cut it.
- **Bot secret handling.** The drafts made an Article 32 security representation ("API keys are encrypted repository secrets and are never committed to source"). Nothing in the audit covers this, and "never committed" is a claim about the entire history of two repositories. Either verify both histories or reduce to what is supported: *the bots run in GitHub Actions as outbound clients and expose no inbound network surface.*
- **Internal Article 30 record (one page, not published).** Article 30(5) exempts organisations under 250 people from keeping a record of processing only where processing is occasional, poses no risk, and involves no special-category data. Inbound correspondence to two published addresses is not occasional. On the regulator reading, the derogation fails for at least the email processing. Create a one-page internal record covering (a) email correspondence and (b) site hosting/IP at the GitHub layer — purposes, categories of data subject and data, recipients (Microsoft, GitHub), transfers, retention, security measures. Keep it available on request. **Do not publish it and add nothing to the policy.**

## 5. Decisions only Thomas can make

Each of these is yours; each has a recommendation.

**1. Does the vote UI ship at all while its numbers are synthetic?**
Trade-off: shipping it shows the product's intended shape and gathers design feedback, but the interface displays fabricated tallies and a fabricated geographic breakdown to real users. A disclosure buried in section 9 of a privacy policy is a weak cure for a number displayed on screen, and it is an Apple Guideline 2.3 (accurate metadata / misleading representation) risk.
**RECOMMENDATION:** either cut the feature from v1, or label the figures as sample data *in the interface itself*, next to the number. Both are cheaper and stronger than the legal drafting currently compensating for it.

**2. Is the vote map persisted to device storage at all before a backend exists?**
Trade-off: persisting it means a reader's votes survive an app restart. Holding it in memory only means they do not. In exchange, holding it in memory removes the entire Article 9 special-category discussion and the ePrivacy Article 5(3) question from the Privacy Policy.
**RECOMMENDATION:** hold it in memory only. This is the largest reduction in compliance surface per unit of effort in the whole package — roughly one line of code — and the feature is not real yet anyway.

**3. The law-enforcement statement (Privacy Policy §12).**
The draft said: *"We have never received a government or law-enforcement request for user data."* Nothing verified that. It is also, functionally, a warrant canary — a statement whose *removal* is the signal — which creates a permanent maintenance obligation. The draft separately asks counsel whether a canary is wise, apparently unaware the preceding line already is one.
**RECOMMENDATION:** delete the sentence and the placeholder both. It costs nothing — the policy already tells readers there is nothing held about app users — and a canary on a project with no server and no logs signals nothing useful while committing you to maintaining it indefinitely.

**4. The admission in Terms §4 that a false "passed a chamber" status was published.**
The draft contained a specific, self-incriminating admission of a past false publication. Nothing in the audit establishes it. Publishing an unverified admission is as damaging as publishing an unverified boast — worse, because it is quotable against you by exactly the audience (law professors) who will check.
**RECOMMENDATION:** if it is true and you want it public, say so and cite the specific post. Otherwise cut to the general, provable claim: *automated classification of legislative stages is genuinely difficult and the Service will contain errors.* Note that the four unfixed US-classifier bugs are a separate engineering matter regardless of what the Terms say.

**5. "Monitored mailbox" and the responsiveness promises in Privacy Policy §16.**
"Monitored" is an implicit commitment to respond, and no triage process exists behind the mailbox. The Article 12(3) one-month response is genuine law and must stay. But *"we can and will act on access, rectification and erasure requests"* and *"write to us and we will consider it on its merits"* are promises you are undertaking, not descriptions of the status quo. This is the same claim the house already softened once ("every report is read").
**RECOMMENDATION:** drop the word "monitored" — *"Both addresses are aliases on a single mailbox and receive mail."* Keep the Article 12(3) statement. Keep the other two only if you are willing to honour them personally; there is no process behind them.

**6. Fix the permalink meta/OG AI-label gap, or publish the disclosure?**
Fixing it is small (see 4.2) and deletes a disclosed defect from two public documents.
**RECOMMENDATION:** fix it before publication.

**7. Publish a postal address, and if so which one?**
Both stores will require a verified address from an individual developer account regardless — that is separate. The question here is what must appear in the *documents*. Publishing a home address is irreversible in practice.
**RECOMMENDATION:** do not publish a home address. Ask counsel the narrow question (counsel question 12) about what identifying information must appear in the Terms as opposed to only in the store listings, and if an address is required, obtain a service address first.

**8. Affirmative acceptance of the Terms, or browsewrap?**
A first-run screen with a link and a tap makes the Terms far more likely to bind. No screen means the Terms are browsewrap and largely informative. This is a build decision that must be made before publication, not after.
**RECOMMENDATION:** given the user pays nothing and receives no service beyond reading public-record information, browsewrap is proportionate. Add a Terms link in Settings next to the Privacy Policy link you already have to build. Revisit if voting ever goes live.

**9. Does the accuracy mailbox get a triage commitment?**
An overrides/corrections mechanism exists in code, but whether a public correction log is live, and at what URL, is not established. Do not promise a public log or "we will review it" until the mechanism and the URL exist. The current wording — "a mechanism exists in the code" — is close to meaningless to a reader.
**RECOMMENDATION:** cut the sentence rather than keep it. Say the address receives corrections and stop. Add the log and the responsiveness language in the same commit that makes the log public.

**10. Bulk or dataset access for academics?**
Decide before someone asks, because the answer shapes the intellectual property section — and the target audience is exactly the group that will ask.
**RECOMMENDATION:** state that the underlying legislative record is public and point to api.congress.gov, OEIL and Cellar, without committing to provide a derived dataset. That answers the question honestly, costs nothing, and preserves the option.

## 6. Questions for counsel

Numbered, with the context needed to answer efficiently. Question 1 unblocks several others.

**1. Trader/consumer status of a zero-revenue individual operator under Polish and EU consumer law.** Thomas is a natural person resident in Poland, no company, none planned. The project earns nothing: no ads, sponsors, purchases, subscriptions, or paid placements, and X creator monetization is neither eligible nor enrolled. Is he a *przedsiębiorca* / trader? **This is question one** — the unfair-terms analysis (Directive 93/13/EEC, Civil Code art. 385¹), the liability section, the e-Commerce Directive address duty and the CalOPPA "commercial" analysis all depend on the same answer. Note: the drafts deliberately avoid asserting "non-commercial" as a conclusion.

**2. Polish Article 85 GDPR derogation.** Poland implemented Article 85 (journalistic, academic, artistic and literary expression) in the Act of 10 May 2018, disapplying substantial parts of the Regulation including Articles 13–16 and 21. Republishing and describing the legislative record about named legislators looks like the paradigm case, and Poland is the applicable jurisdiction. Does it cover this processing? If yes, the Article 14 notification problem for legislator data largely disappears and the disproportionate-effort analysis the drafts reach for is unnecessary. **This is likely the cheapest large win in the package.**

**3. Does the DSA apply at all?** The drafts assume it does and only ask about classification within it — building a full Article 14 content-restriction policy, dual Article 11/12 points of contact, and a policy section around it. But the DSA applies to *intermediary services* (Art 3(g): mere conduit, caching, hosting). BillTracking publishes exclusively its own content and, as built, stores nothing supplied by a recipient — there is no server and nothing is transmitted. If scope is not engaged, Articles 11, 12 and 14 do not attach at all, and not because of any micro-enterprise exemption (Art 19 only exempts Section 3 platform duties). Confirm. If correct, delete the DSA framing and keep a short acceptable-use section on ordinary contract grounds plus one contact address. **Re-run scope if reader votes are ever submitted and stored — stored reader-submitted content may make this a hosting service, at which point Arts 14, 16, 17 and 20 need drafting.**

**4. Verify every legal citation independently.** The audit verified source code, not law. Specifically: *Breyer* C-582/14; *EDPS v SRB* C-413/23 P and its stated 4 September 2025 date; EDPB draft Guidelines 02/2026 on anonymisation; Cohen & Nissim, *PNAS* 2020; AI Act Art 50(2) and 50(4) and the 2 August 2026 application date; 17 U.S.C. § 105. The audience is law professors, who will check citations before they check anything else.

**5. *EDPS v SRB* specifically — do not accept the drafter's characterisation.** The draft cites it for the proposition that anonymity is assessed from the *controller's own* perspective at the moment of collection. The SRB litigation is best known for close to the opposite: the General Court in T-557/20 held that pseudonymised data may not be personal data *in the hands of a recipient* lacking the means to re-identify — a recipient-relative test. Please read the actual judgment. Either supply the correct authority or state the proposition without a case name. Same treatment for the EDPB guidelines number, which cannot be verified here.

**6. Article 9 availability for the planned anonymous vote.** The draft asserted that if a submitted vote were held to be personal data, *"we would have no Article 9(2) condition available"*. That is too strong: Art 9(2)(a) explicit consent exists in the abstract, and Art 9(2)(e) (manifestly made public by the data subject) is at least arguable. The narrower and probably correct position is that **no Article 9(2) condition is compatible with the chosen anonymous design**, because explicit consent must be demonstrable under Art 7(1) and demonstrability requires an identifier the design deliberately removes. Confirm, and confirm the consequence: the feature would need redesign, not a rewritten justification.

**7. Recital 26 anonymity versus Article 11.** The drafts pick one theory (anonymous data outside the GDPR, on the Recital 26 basis) and never name the second, which a regulator may reach for first. Article 11 presupposes the data *is* personal data but does not require identification: it relieves the controller of Arts 15–20 while imposing Art 11(2) duties (inform the data subject you cannot identify them; permit them to supply identifying information). The planned payload — bill reference, position, self-reported country and US state, plus any timestamp — looks like the classic Article 11 case, particularly in small geographic cells where the differencing leak in 4.1 makes singling out easier rather than harder. **Which framing should the design be built to survive? Decide before anything is built.**

**8. Is the operator a controller for data written to a user's own device by software he authored but cannot read?** Storage is expressly processing under Art 4(2), and controllership does not require access to the data (EDPB Guidelines 07/2020). The favourable answer — no access, no personal data in his hands — is good but contested. The drafts currently give two different confidence levels on this same question in two sections; please settle it once.

**9. ePrivacy Article 5(3) and the vote map.** Does the strictly-necessary exemption cover writing a *political opinion* to the device, as opposed to a display preference? The draft proposed a one-time in-app disclosure at first vote as the fix. That looks wrong: Art 5(3) is a **consent** rule, not a transparency rule, so the cure would be consent (freely given, specific, informed, unambiguous, affirmative) — or removing the vote map from persisted storage and holding it in memory only. Please confirm which. (See owner decision 2; the memory-only route may moot this question entirely.)

**10. Article 6 basis for republishing the legislative record.** The drafts blend Art 6(1)(f) legitimate interests with "the public interest" in a single cell. Art 6(1)(e) is a distinct basis requiring a foundation in Union or Member State law and is not being relied on. Confirm the correct formulation — presumably Art 6(1)(f) alone, with the public interest characterised as the interest pursued within the balancing test.

**11. Processor and transfer position for GitHub Pages (free account) and Microsoft Exchange Online.** Does GitHub Pages on a free account come with Article 28(3) processor terms? What transfer mechanism does each rely on? If GitHub offers no Art 28 terms, is that a gap to record and accept, or a reason to change host? Context: no server exists anywhere in the project; GitHub does not surface visitor access logs to repository owners, but as host it necessarily processes visitor IP addresses at the network layer. **The drafts must not claim that no IP processing occurs.**

**12. Identifying information in the documents.** The draft framed the address duty under the Consumer Rights Directive. That looks like the wrong instrument: the CRD's Art 6 duties attach to distance *contracts* for goods, services or digital content — including, since Directive 2019/2161, digital content supplied against personal data — and here nothing is supplied against payment or against personal data, since the app transmits nothing. The likelier hook is Article 5 of the e-Commerce Directive 2000/31/EC and its Polish implementation, whose scope test (information society service) is different and also arguable for a zero-revenue service. **Narrow question: what identifying information must appear in the Terms themselves, as opposed to only in the store listings?** (See owner decision 7 — please do not assume a home address is acceptable.)

**13. Mandatory liability carve-outs under Polish law.** The draft's carve-out list was imported from an English-law template and omits Civil Code art. 473 §2, which voids in advance any exclusion of liability for intentionally caused damage (*szkoda wyrządzona umyślnie*). Since the operator is a natural person in Poland with uncapped personal exposure and Polish courts are the realistic forum, this is the one carve-out that must be present. Please confirm the full Polish list, and advise whether any liability cap is worth stating where the user pays nothing.

**14. Governing law and jurisdiction.** Undecided in the draft, deliberately. The draft's suggested shape — Polish law and courts, with an express carve-out preserving EU consumers' mandatory protections and home forum, and nothing purporting to waive non-waivable US state rights — is offered as a starting proposal to accept or reject.

**15. Unfair-terms and the Polish register reference.** Directive 93/13/EEC and Civil Code art. 385¹ apply only trader-to-consumer, so this section stands or falls with question 1. Separately, the draft's reference to the Polish *register of prohibited clauses* is dated — UOKiK has proceeded by administrative decision rather than register entry since the April 2016 reform. Please update to abusive-clause decisions.

**16. Terms §13 / Privacy Policy consistency on the site.** One draft says "the website collects nothing from anyone", which contradicts the GitHub IP concession elsewhere in the same package. Confirm the correct formulation and use it **identically** in all three places. The proposed wording: *the website sets nothing on your device and we receive no visitor logs.*

**17. US state privacy laws — is any of this needed?** The draft asserted in the operator's voice that no enacted US state comprehensive privacy law treats political opinions as sensitive personal information — a sweeping negative across roughly twenty statutes, unverified. Please confirm or correct. And please confirm the simpler prior point: the operator processes no US resident's personal data at all through the app (transmits nothing) or the site (no scripts, forms, cookies, or logs reachable by him), so most of these statutes never reach their threshold question. Note that the drafts apply the stricter European standard everywhere as a design choice regardless.

**18. AI Act Article 50 deployer analysis.** Confirm that the visible `[AI-Generated]` label, in the form used, satisfies the Art 50(2) duty; confirm the provider/deployer split as stated; and confirm that the second-subparagraph human-editorial-review exemption in Art 50(4) is **unavailable** on these facts, since the bots publish unreviewed on a schedule. Note the disclosed meta-tag gap in 4.2 above.

**19. Source attribution wording.** The exact attribution required by the Congress.gov API terms and by the EU institutions' reuse conditions (Commission Decision 2011/833/EU and the European Parliament reuse notice), to be stated **verbatim** in the documents rather than paraphrased.

**20. Email retention period.** A figure the operator will actually apply to inbound correspondence at contact@ and accuracy@. Please propose one rather than leaving it open.

**21. Right to object — placement.** Article 21(4) requires the right to object to be brought to the data subject's attention explicitly and separately from other information, at the latest at the time of first communication. Burying it in a bulleted list of rights alongside portability does not satisfy that. Confirm it should be a standalone bolded paragraph ahead of the rights list.

**22. Minimum age, Apple age rating and IARC answers.** Bill summaries will inevitably touch violence, drugs and sexual-offence subject matter because legislation does. What is the intended minimum age and what do the rating questionnaires require?

## 8. If analytics is added later

`site/ANALYTICS-DECISION.md` recommends possibly adding **Cloudflare Web Analytics** at some point — cookieless, no device storage, no identifier. **It is not installed.** There is currently no analytics of any kind on billtracking.org, and these documents are written for that state. If it is ever adopted, exactly one thing must change: a new paragraph in the Privacy Policy's website section, alongside the GitHub-hosting paragraph, disclosing that the site loads a script from Cloudflare, that the script sets nothing on the device and assigns no identifier, that Cloudflare processes the request as a processor, and naming its transfer mechanism. The "the website runs no JavaScript and loads nothing from any other company" sentence in the plain-English summary would have to change at the same time — that sentence is currently the site's strongest claim and adding any third-party script retires it. Nothing else in either document is affected. **Cloudflare must not be named anywhere in the published documents until this is actually executed** — naming it now implies a vendor relationship that does not exist.
